Drugs

Understanding drug use and finding support

There are many different classifications of drugs (e.g. opioids, stimulants, psychedelics etc.) and drugs in each classification affect people in different ways. Going through this in detail is beyond the scope of this resource but you can find reliable information about this on the Talk to Frank website.

Impact on Studies: Drug use can have severe consequences on academic performance, affecting concentration and motivation. If you're witnessing a decline in your studies due to drug use, seeking help is essential.

Concern for Others: Approach a friend or family member with care if you suspect drug-related challenges as the person may find this a sensitive subject and may be worried about the implications of talking about this with others. Encourage open, non-judgmental communication, express your support, and guide them toward professional assistance.

Recognising a Problem

As with alcohol use, a common mental defence is "everyone does it"… but a useful question to ask is 'does everyone experience the same problems or consequences that I do?'. Some people seem to be able to control their drug use to some extent, while others find this difficult or impossible. Within the anonymous fellowships (e.g. Narcotics Anonymous) there is a belief that once a person has lost control of their drug use they have developed a chronic condition that can only be safely managed by abstaining from drug use.

  • Is your drug use or recovering from drug use impacting on your work or studies (days off, missed deadlines, falling behind, disciplinary action)?
  • Are you spending more than you can afford/more than you would like on drugs?
  • Have you or someone else been injured as a result of your drug consumption?
  • Do you often fail to do what would otherwise be expected of you because of your drug use?
  • Do you sometimes feel guilt, remorse or shame after taking drugs?
  • Has anyone expressed concern about your drug use or suggested that you cut down/stop?

If the answer to any of these questions is 'yes' then this may indicate that your drug use is problematic and it may be time to seek help.

What You Can Do

Professional help is available through counselling services, Alcohol & Drugs Action or through your GP. Engage in self-help by seeking alternative, adaptive coping mechanisms and establishing a support network. Taking proactive steps can pave the way for a healthier lifestyle.

  • Consider whether you know exactly what it is that you are taking.
  • Try to make sure that someone knows where you are when you are using drugs and that they can check on your safety.
  • Use a small amount first and allow plenty of time for the drug to take effect before considering taking any more.
  • Pace yourself and don't feel pressured by having to keep up with friends. Monitor your water intake if taking empathogens.
  • Be aware of the possible dangers of polydrug use (using a number of different drugs in combination). E.g. using combinations involving alcohol, benzodiazepines and/or opioids can increase the risk of overdose.
  • Be aware of how to support someone if you witness an overdose. If you are likely to be around people who are using opioids you should carry Naloxone (opioid overdose reversal drug). You can find out about this and how to access free take-home Naloxone here.
  • Set limits on what you will use, how much and for how long.
  • Monitor your own drug use.
  • Seek support from friends and/or family.
