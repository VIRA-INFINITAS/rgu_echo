Alcohol

Understanding your relationship with alcohol

Are you questioning your alcohol consumption? Recognising a potential issue involves considering how your drinking patterns may be affecting your well-being. Symptoms may include increased tolerance, neglecting responsibilities, or experiencing negative consequences.

Impact on Studies: Excessive alcohol use can disrupt academic performance, impairing focus and memory. If you're noticing a decline in your studies due to alcohol, it's time to take action.

Concern for Others: If you observe a friend or family member struggling with alcohol, express your concern with empathy. Encourage open dialogue and provide support while suggesting professional assistance when needed. If you suspect that someone may have developed a dependence on alcohol do not encourage them to stop using alcohol abruptly as this can be very dangerous. Instead, the person should seek professional help with reducing and then stopping alcohol use in order to reduce the risk of alcohol withdrawal seizures. If you are with someone who has passed out/fallen asleep after drinking heavily you should try to make the area around them safe and place them in the recovery position so as to reduce the risk of them choking on their own vomit.

Recognising a Problem

  • Is your drinking/recovering from drinking impacting on your work or studies (days off, missed deadlines, falling behind, disciplinary action)?
  • Are you spending more than you can afford/more than you would like on alcohol?
  • Are you regularly drinking more than the UK government's low risk drinking guidelines?
  • Have you or someone else been injured as a result of your drinking?
  • Do you ever feel the need to have a drink of alcohol on the morning after a night of drinking?
  • Do you often fail to do what would otherwise be expected of you because of your drinking?
  • Do you sometimes feel guilt, remorse or shame after drinking?
  • Do you have 'blackouts' (times where you cannot remember what you did when you were drinking)?
  • Has anyone expressed concern about your drinking or suggested that you cut down/stop?

If the answer to any of these questions is 'yes' then this may indicate that your alcohol use is problematic and it may be time to seek help.

What You Can Do

Professional help is available through your GP, through third-sector organisations such as Alcohol and Drugs Action or through counselling services.

  • Decide how much money you are going to take with you and leave your bank card at home.
  • Never walk home alone and keep enough money for a taxi.
  • Drink on a full stomach. It won't stop the alcohol reaching your bloodstream but it will slow it down.
  • Pace yourself and don't feel pressured by having to keep up with friends. Have water in-between to slow the process down.
  • Watch out for drink spiking. Keep an eye on your drink and never leave it unattended.
  • Set limits on what you will drink, how much and for how long
  • Monitor your own drinking
  • Seek support from friends and/or family
