Traumatic Events

Support for processing difficult experiences

Sudden trauma or traumatic events can significantly destract you from concentrating on your course work and student everyday life. Dealing with traumatic events can include loss of sleep, panicking, missing your classes, poor engagement in course activities, confusion, unwanted thoughts or images, feeling tense, 'disorientated', difficulties in relationships, lack of motivation, suicidal thoughts in some severe occasions, etc.

What are traumatic events?

Sudden trauma or traumatic events can be any extreme or unforeseen events that could cause a traumatic reaction such as shock, panicking, not knowing what to do, etc. Traumatic events include events such as military conflicts, natural or industrial disasters, death of a close relative or friend, terminal illness but can also include:

  • an assault of any kind - physical, sexual or psychological
  • parent's divorce
  • loss of a job or other source of income
  • car accidents and/or severe injuries due to a car accident
  • being threatened with harm or death
  • witnessing an event that caused someone harm or death
  • being harassed through the social media

Recognising a problem

Recognising if you've experienced trauma can be complex as it can manifest differently in individuals. However, some common signs that might indicate you've been traumatized include:

  • Recurrent Intrusive Memories: Flashbacks, nightmares, or distressing memories about the traumatic event that intrude into your thoughts.
  • Avoidance: Avoiding situations, places, people, or conversations that remind you of the traumatic event.
  • Emotional Distress: Persistent feelings of fear, sadness, anger, guilt, or shame related to the event.
  • Hypervigilance: Being constantly on edge, feeling easily startled, or having a heightened sense of awareness as if always on guard.
  • Negative Changes in Thinking and Mood: Feeling detached, losing interest in previously enjoyable activities, having difficulty concentrating, or feeling numb.
  • Physical Symptoms: Experiencing physical reactions such as headaches, stomachaches, or other unexplained pains.
  • Sleep Disturbances: Difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or experiencing nightmares related to the traumatic event.
  • Changes in Relationships: Struggling with trust, feeling isolated, or having difficulties in maintaining relationships.

It's important to note that the impact of trauma can vary widely among individuals. Not everyone who experiences a traumatic event will develop symptoms of trauma, and the severity and duration of symptoms can also differ.

If you suspect you've been traumatised and these symptoms persist, it may be helpful to seek support from a mental health professional. Therapy, counseling, or trauma-focused interventions can assist in processing and coping with the effects of trauma. Talking to a trusted friend, family member, or support group can also be beneficial in navigating these experiences.

What you can do

Talking to others can be very helpful and is an important way of making connections. If family or friends are supportive, tell them about your experience. It is also important to take care of yourself by acknowledging that you have been exposed to an unexpected trauma.

You may consider speaking to your Personal Tutor and they will be able to put into place extra measures to ensure your studies are not affected.

You may also wish to speak with the counselling service to get help with coming to terms with what has happened by calling 01224 262120 or emailing counselling@rgu.ac.uk.

Whatever your reactions are keep reminding yourself that your responses are normal responses to a stressful situation. Give yourself permission to do whatever you need to do to take care of yourself and seek professional help.
