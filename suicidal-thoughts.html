Suicidal Thoughts

You are not alone - help and support are available

Crisis Support - Available Now

Samaritans (24/7): 116 123
Emergency: 999

Concern for others

If you think that a friend or peer may be struggling with suicidal thoughts you can try to talk with them about it sensitively. Remember that the person may feel guilty or embarrassed about their situation so discussion that is rooted in empathic understanding is important. You can encourage the person to speak with their personal tutor at university and/or to seek help from a health professional.

If you are concerned that the person is thinking about death or suicide then you should ask them specifically about this if you feel able to. People often worry that asking about suicidal thoughts will 'put the idea into the person's head', however the opposite is true… if a person is not thinking about suicide then asking them about it will not make them start considering this as an option. If a person is thinking about suicide then they often feel like this is not something they can discuss with others due to the taboo nature of the topic, but you asking them about it can make it feel safe to discuss and can allow the person to share how they feel rather than struggling with this alone.

Recognising a Problem

  • Frequency and Intensity: If thoughts of self-harm or suicide are frequent, persistent, and intense, especially if they worsen over time.
  • Preoccupation: Constantly thinking about death, dying, or harming oneself.
  • Hopelessness: Feeling a deep sense of hopelessness or feeling like there's no way out of a situation.
  • Isolation: Withdrawing from friends, family, or social activities, and feeling disconnected from others.
  • Giving Away Possessions: Making plans or giving away prized possessions, as if preparing for one's death.
  • Talking or Writing About Death: Talking or writing about death, dying, or suicide, even in a subtle or vague manner.
  • Sudden Mood Changes: Experiencing sudden shifts in mood, such as from extreme despair to unexpected calmness, which might indicate a decision to act on suicidal thoughts.
  • Substance Use: Increased use or reliance on drugs or alcohol as a way to cope with emotional pain.

What you can do

If you are concerned that you may be in immediate danger of harming yourself or killing yourself contact the emergency services or one of the crisis lines detailed on this page.

If you or someone you know is experiencing these signs or expressing suicidal thoughts, it's essential to take it seriously and seek help. Contact a mental health professional, call emergency services, or reach out to a trusted friend or family member for support. There are hotlines and crisis intervention services available that offer immediate help and guidance. Remember, these feelings are serious and there are people ready to help.
