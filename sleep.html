Sleep

Better sleep for better mental wellbeing

We require sleep to maintain our mental and physical wellbeing. When it's hard to sleep- try focussing on rest and letting go of the day rather than 'trying to sleep'. When we let the energy of anxiety or excitement out of the body, then sleep comes much more easily. The amount of sleep we need changes throughout our lifetime and most adults need on average seven to eight hours sleep per night. Difficulty sleeping or having disturbed sleep patterns could be due to stress or mental health problems. Lack of sleep occurring at exam time is normal for most people and a regular sleep pattern should resume once they are over. However, lack of sleep over a prolonged period of time can lead to fatigue and affect your concentration, or it may be a cause or a symptom of mental illness such as anxiety / depression or a physical ailment.

Recognising a Problem

There are several signs that may indicate you have a sleep problem:

  • Difficulty Falling Asleep: If you regularly struggle to fall asleep within 15-30 minutes of lying down.
  • Frequent Awakening: Waking up multiple times during the night and having trouble falling back asleep.
  • Daytime Fatigue: Feeling excessively tired, drowsy, or lacking energy during the day, even after what should be a full night's sleep.
  • Irritability or Mood Changes: Sleep deprivation can lead to irritability, mood swings, or difficulty concentrating.
  • Snoring or Breathing Pauses: If you or someone notices loud snoring or you experience moments of paused breathing during sleep, it might indicate sleep apnoea.
  • Restless Legs: Feeling uncomfortable sensations in your legs, leading to an urge to move them, especially when lying down.
  • Sleepwalking or Talking: Engaging in activities during sleep without awareness.
  • Frequent Napping: Feeling the need to take regular naps during the day to compensate for inadequate nighttime sleep.

If you notice persistent patterns of these symptoms that interfere with your daily life, it might be worth discussing with a healthcare professional to evaluate whether there's an underlying sleep disorder or issue that needs attention.

What you can do

  • Go to bed when you are ready to sleep
  • Avoid using your computer or watching television in bed
  • If you can't sleep after being in bed a short while, get up and try having a milky drink
  • Don't begin to reflect on your day before bedtime
  • Don't sleep in late to catch up after a disturbed night
  • Have a warm bath
  • Get enough exercise during the daytime

Tracking your sleep patterns and habits using a sleep diary can also provide valuable information for discussions with a doctor.
