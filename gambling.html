Gambling

Recognising and addressing gambling problems

Compulsive gambling can lead to financial strain and emotional distress. Gambling can quickly become extremely time consuming as the person who is gambling chases further wins or tries to recoup losses. If you find yourself unable to control gambling habits, it's time to consider the possibility of a problem.

The consequences of gambling can extend to academic performance. Time that is earmarked for study can quickly disappear playing online casino, slots or bingo games. Money that is needed for paying rent, buying food or university supplies can disappear just as quickly when spread betting. Recognise the signs and seek help before it affects your studies.

If someone close to you is struggling with gambling, express your concern and guide them toward the support they need. Encourage them to seek professional assistance for a path to recovery.

Recognising a Problem

  • Do you borrow money to gamble with?
  • Do you find yourself missing or cancelling appointments/commitments so that you can continue gambling?
  • Have you ever promised anyone that you would stop gambling but then continued?
  • Do you ever gamble for longer than you intended and keep going until all of your money is gone?
  • Do you find yourself gambling in order to try to recoup losses or solve financial difficulties?
  • Have you ever done things you were not proud of or committed illegal acts in order to get money to gamble with?
  • Have you lied to cover up how much money you have gambled?
  • Have you experienced despair or remorse as a result of your gambling?

If the answer to any of these questions is 'yes' then this may indicate that your gambling is problematic and it may be time to seek help.

What You Can Do

Seek professional help through counselling services and consider self-help measures such as setting limits and finding alternative activities. Breaking the cycle is essential for regaining control.

  • Ask gambling companies to stop serving you (self-exclusion)
  • Ask your bank to limit how much you can spend on gambling by blocking payments to gambling companies or putting a temporary freeze on your card spending
  • Use software to block gambling websites
  • Talk to someone about your gambling (see links to support)
  • Talk to family and/or friends
  • Get help with debt (see links to support)
  • Check if you can get a refund if you borrowed money (E.g. if your bank should have known you were gambling before lending you money as your bank statements showed that you were gambling)
  • Complain to the Gambling Commission – if you were allowed to gamble after you self-excluded
